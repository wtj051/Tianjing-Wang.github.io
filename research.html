Research

Trustworthy AI for grid-battery-data-center systems

My research develops physics-guided AI and optimization methods for energy systems where decisions must be fast, data-driven, and physically accountable. The central question is how AI can support clean-energy infrastructure while respecting feasibility, safety, device health, uncertainty, and operational accountability.

Central research question. How can AI make reliable decisions for clean-energy infrastructure when grid physics, battery degradation, flexible computing demand, uncertainty, and real-time operational constraints interact?

01

Pillar 01

Physics-trustworthy AI for safe grid operation

Modern power grids increasingly operate near dynamic limits because of renewable variability, EV charging, distributed energy resources, and extreme events. My work develops AI methods that embed power-system constraints into learning and control models rather than treating physical feasibility as an afterthought.

Technical signature

  • Power-flow-aware learning and feasibility restoration.
  • Graph and time-series models for grid topology, states, and contingencies.
  • Safe reinforcement learning for emergency voltage control and redispatch.
  • Knowledge-guided models that combine operator logic with data-driven adaptation.

Evaluation background

Average error is not enough for grid operation. A useful model should be evaluated by voltage/security violations, power-flow solvability, contingency robustness, runtime, and ability to generalize across grid conditions.

02

Pillar 02

Life-aware battery intelligence

Battery degradation changes the amount of flexibility that can be safely used. My work connects battery SOH/SOC/RUL and future-capacity estimation with degradation-aware dispatch, grid-edge planning, and operational decision support.

Technical signature

  • Physics-guided deep learning for future capacity and remaining useful life.
  • Continuous-time and irregular-sampling models for real battery data.
  • Weakly supervised learning under noisy or imprecise health labels.
  • Health-to-dispatch features: safe capacity, power margin, degradation cost, and lifecycle risk.

Evaluation background

Battery models should be evaluated by prediction accuracy, uncertainty calibration, early-life generalization, robustness to heterogeneous aging, and downstream dispatch consequences—not only by curve-fitting quality.

03

Pillar 03

Data-center energy intelligence

AI computing is rapidly increasing data-center electricity demand, but data centers also contain controllable flexibility: workload scheduling, on-site storage, cooling/thermal inertia, renewable procurement, and demand response. My emerging work studies data centers as grid-interactive energy assets rather than fixed loads.

Technical signature

  • Endogenous modeling of AI-driven computing demand and flexible workload timing.
  • Uncertainty quantification for workload, renewable output, electricity price, and grid stress.
  • Coordinated planning of energy storage and flexible data-center demand.
  • Multi-timescale operation across workload scheduling, battery dispatch, cooling flexibility, and grid-support response.

Evaluation background

Data-center energy AI should be judged by total cost, peak-load reduction, renewable utilization, service-quality violations, storage degradation, thermal feasibility, and robustness under demand and price uncertainty.
