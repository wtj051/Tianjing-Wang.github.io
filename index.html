Independent research program

Three connected research pillars

My work studies energy systems where fast data-driven decisions must remain compatible with physics, hardware health, and real operational constraints. The three pillars below form a coherent lab vision: safe infrastructure, life-aware devices, and flexible computing demand.

Pillar 01

Safe grid operation

AI for power-flow feasibility, voltage/security constraints, emergency control, and resilient operation under renewable, load, and contingency uncertainty.

Physics-guided learning Graph/time-series models Reinforcement learning Security-constrained dispatch

Background. As grids integrate more inverter-based renewables, EV charging, and distributed storage, operators need fast decision support that respects non-negotiable physical limits rather than optimizing average prediction accuracy alone.

Pillar 02

Life-aware battery intelligence

Battery SOH/SOC/RUL, future-capacity prediction, degradation-aware dispatch, and health-to-value decision tools for storage, EVs, and grid-edge assets.

SOH / SOC / RUL Future capacity Degradation risk Health-aware dispatch

Background. Batteries are not passive energy buffers. Their aging state determines available flexibility, operational risk, warranty cost, and long-term value; AI models should therefore connect health estimation to dispatch and planning decisions.

Pillar 03

Data-center energy intelligence

Learning and optimization for AI-driven data-center demand, storage planning, cooling flexibility, renewable integration, and grid-aware workload coordination.

Flexible computing demand Storage + workload co-optimization Cooling flexibility Uncertainty-aware planning

Background. AI computing is turning data centers into large and increasingly dynamic energy assets. Treating computing load as fixed demand misses opportunities for grid support, peak reduction, renewable utilization, and storage value stacking.

Research ecosystem

From prediction to physically accountable decision-making

The unifying theme is to move beyond black-box prediction toward AI systems that are benchmarked against physical constraints, device-health constraints, uncertainty stress tests, and reproducibility requirements.

  • Grid decisions must satisfy power-flow and security constraints.
  • Battery decisions must preserve health, safety margins, and lifetime value.
  • Data-center decisions must coordinate workload, storage, cooling, and grid interaction across timescales.
  • Benchmarks should report failure modes, not only average performance.
